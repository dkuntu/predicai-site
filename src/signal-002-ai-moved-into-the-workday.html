01 Mandate

If you only read one thing

Stop treating AI like something you’ll “learn later.”

This week, AI didn’t show up as a headline. It showed up inside normal work: meetings, documents, notes, and follow-ups.

Start here:
Use AI inside one real task you already do — and turn it into a small learning loop.

Here’s what’s happening quietly:
AI is becoming part of the routine.
That’s when skill gaps show up.

Not because people are “behind.”
Because the work moved.
Quick rule

If you can’t explain what the tool did, don’t let it make the call.

02 What changed

The change wasn’t a smarter model. It was where AI showed up.

Instead of “open a chatbot and ask,” more tools are quietly helping with: meeting recap, action items, follow-up drafts, and document cleanup.

  • Concrete detail: meeting notes becoming “raw material” you can search and reuse.
  • What it replaces: re-reading threads and rebuilding context every time you come back.
  • So what: learning AI becomes less about features — and more about habits.
  • Skeptic line: if you don’t review what AI captured, you can repeat mistakes faster.
Plain-English translation:
AI is moving from “something you open” to “something that sits inside the work.”

03 What I tested / reviewed

Across the week, one pattern stood out: people aren’t asking “What can AI do?” They’re asking “How do I keep up while doing my actual job?”

Where it showed up most clearly

  • Meetings: summaries and action items becoming the default.
  • Documents: AI editing for clarity before anyone “starts writing.”
  • Follow-ups: drafts appearing instantly — even when the human isn’t ready.
  • Safety: more questions about what you should never upload.

What held up

  • AI is most useful as a review partner, not a replacement voice.
  • People get the best results when they ask for gaps, missing steps, and weak assumptions.

What broke (or easily could)

  • If you accept “clean” output without checking it, you can make the wrong move with confidence.

What surprised me

  • People trust meeting recaps faster than documents — even though meetings are usually messier.

04 One move to steal

This week’s move is simple: turn one real task into a learning loop.

Pick one thing you already do: a follow-up email, a weekly plan, a report, a customer note, a meeting recap.

Explain this task back to me in plain language. What are you assuming? What would make this go wrong? Now ask me 2 questions before you help.
  • Success condition: you can summarize the decision + next step in 2 sentences, and they match what the team actually agreed.
  • Failure signal: you can’t tell what the AI assumed, or you can’t explain the output to someone else.

Why this matters (Academy logic)

This is the exact skill the Academy builds: using AI inside your job, without guessing, and without letting “auto” become “wrong but official.”

05 AI outside the chat box

This is the bigger shift: AI isn’t becoming “a place you go.” It’s becoming the default helper inside tools you already use.

Meetings → searchable memory Workday

Your meeting doesn’t end when the call ends anymore. It turns into notes, tasks, and follow-ups — automatically.

  • Behavior change: your next step is pre-drafted.
  • Judgment: Held up
  • Skeptic line: review first. Don’t let “auto” become “wrong but official.”
Documents → living drafts Workday

Instead of writing from scratch, more people are using AI to tighten clarity, find missing steps, and clean structure before they add content.

  • Behavior change: AI becomes an editor, not a ghostwriter.
  • Judgment: Keeper
  • Skeptic line: if the source is weak, the edit will be weak.
Coding helpers → supervised speed Coding

One example this week: people using Claude to read existing code, explain logic, and suggest cleaner versions — with human review.

  • Behavior change: faster understanding, not blind copy/paste.
  • Judgment: Held up
  • Skeptic line: if you can’t explain the change, don’t ship it.
Safety questions → louder on real files Safety

More people are asking the right question: “Should I upload this file at all?” Especially when it contains private or sensitive info.

  • Behavior change: a “can I upload this?” pause becomes a habit.
  • Judgment: Still building
  • Skeptic line: “it’s probably fine” is not a policy.
Simple rule:
If you’d feel weird forwarding it to the wrong person, don’t upload it into a tool you don’t fully control.
Plain-English translation:
“AI outside the chat box” = tools that change habits without you learning a new app.
